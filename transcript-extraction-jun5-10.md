### Risk fundamentals (Ch 1)
- Risk pooling = loss sharing to ease burden on one individual (sorority/$10 example).
- Law of large numbers = predict losses with many similar risks; large representative sample. (Contrast: fallacy of small numbers — penny-stock % example.)
- Risk = **uncertainty regarding financial loss**. Insurance restores insured to prior financial condition — not for gain, not an investment.
- Speculative risk (loss OR gain — stocks, gambling, new business, junk bonds) = NOT insurable. Pure risk (loss only) = the only insurable risk.
- Peril = the **cause** of loss (life: death; health: injury/illness).
- Hazards increase likelihood of loss: **physical** (icy walk, broken stairs, blindness, deafness, obesity), **moral** (lifestyle/tendencies — smoking, drugs, alcohol, tendency to steal), **morale** (attitude/indifference — no helmet, no seatbelt).
- Risk management acronym **A-R-R-T**: Avoidance (never fly), Reduction (ring in safe-deposit box), Retention (self-insurance), Transference (purchase insurance). Buying insurance = transferring risk.
- Elements of insurable risk: due to chance; definite & measurable (time and place); predictable; **not catastrophic**; large exposure (good risks offset bad); randomly selected (prevents adverse selection). **[EXAM]** "Loss must be catastrophic" is the wrong-answer trap on NOT questions.
- "Principle of large loss": insure large losses, not small claims.
- Human life value = your personal economic worth / future earnings to dependents.
- Adverse selection = the sick disproportionately seeking coverage / concealing illness. Lapsed-policy reinstatement requires back premiums + health questionnaire because reinstatement attracts adverse selection. Info checked via **MIB (Medical Information Bureau)**.

### Insurer types (Ch 2)
- **Stock insurer**: owned by stockholders; dividends to stockholders; sells **non-participating** policies only. **[EXAM]**
- **Mutual insurer**: owned by policyholders (no stock, not public); elect board (proxy votes); **participating** policies; dividends to policyholders (reduce premium / cash value); **dividends are NOT taxable — return of premium**; premiums usually slightly higher. **Demutualization** = mutual → stock.
- **Reciprocal insurer**: ownership by policyholders pooling resources; usually managed by an attorney(-in-fact); not common.
- **Reinsurer**: business-to-business only. Transferring company = **ceding** (C-E-D-I-N-G) company; accepting company = reinsurer. **[EXAM]**
- **Fraternal benefit society** (p. 17) **[EXAM]**: must be (1) nonprofit, (2) lodge/large system with **ritualistic work**, (3) elected officers / representative form of government, (4) organized under special insurance-code section. Issues certificates. Example: Knights of Columbus.
- **Lloyd's of London is NOT an insurer** (p. 17) — entity bringing individuals/companies into syndicates to underwrite unusual risks (compares to NYSE; "Costco membership" analogy). **[EXAM]**
- **Risk retention group** (p. 17) **[EXAM]**: a *mutual* company formed to insure people in the **same business/occupation/profession** (dentists, pharmacists, engineers). NOT different occupations.
- **Home service / debit / industrial insurance**: small face amounts ($1,000–$2,000), premiums collected **weekly by the agent at the home**; now illegal/obsolete.
- **Service providers**: HMOs/PPOs; policyholder called a **subscriber**. A private insurer offers **many lines** of insurance.
- **Government (social) insurers**: Social Security (OASDI — Old Age, Survivors & Disability Insurance), Medicare, Medicaid.
- **Self-insurers**: establish their own reserves = risk **retention**.

### Distribution & regulation (Ch 2)
- Agent (producer — "produces business") represents the **insurer**; broker represents the **insured/buyer**. Both must be **appointed**. Money given to the agent = given to the company (agent is a fiduciary; deliver immediately, give receipt, never commingle).
- Career agency = **captive** agents (sell only that company's products). General agent: appointed to a geographic area, recruits/trains, receives overrides. **PPGA** (personal producing general agent): works independently, pays own office/staff, doesn't recruit. Independent agent: sells for many companies but must be appointed by each.
- Broker license = separate license, usually after ~2 years licensed; broker can hire agents and earns overrides.
- **Direct selling / direct underwriters**: no commissioned agent (mail/call center; e.g., NY Life–AARP). You still must be **licensed** to sell, commission or not.
- **McCarran-Ferguson Act**: returned insurance regulation to the **states** (memory hook: "MF = state"). SEC regulates **variable** products (stock-market-based) alongside the state.
- **Fair Credit Reporting Act (1970)**: protects an individual's **right to privacy**; non-compliant companies are liable (fined). Detail in Ch 9.
- **Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act 1999** (book may call it Financial Services Modernization Act, p. 22): repealed Glass-Steagall; banks ↔ insurance **cross-selling** allowed; regulated on a **functional** basis; data-safeguard requirements; anti-money-laundering intent.
- **Domestic** insurer: incorporated and doing business in same state. **Foreign**: incorporated in another **state**. **Alien**: incorporated in another **country**. **[EXAM]**
- Licensing (FL DFS — Department of Financial Services issues licenses; CFO heads it):
  - License is **perpetual** unless suspended/revoked.
  - **CE: 24 hours every 2 years if licensed <6 years (1–5 yrs); 20 hours if 6+ years; 4 hours of ethics mandatory** for everyone. CEs follow the agent, not the company. **[EXAM]**
  - License **terminated if 4 years lapse without an appointment** for each class of insurance on the license.
  - Non-resident license: already licensed in home state → pay fee, **no new exam**. **[EXAM]**
  - U.S. citizenship NOT required; must be at least 18.
- **NAIC** (National Association of Insurance Commissioners): each state's commissioner is a member; develops **guidelines, model legislation, recommendations** (states may or may not adopt); promotes **uniformity**; preserves state regulation; **cannot make law, investigate, issue injunctions, or arrest** (only judges issue injunctions). Created the **Advertising Code** and the **Unfair Trade Practices Act** — all states adopted these. **[EXAM]**
- **Advertising Code**: regulates misleading words/phrases, testimonials, statistics, full disclosure of policy renewal/cancellation/termination provisions.
- **Unfair Trade Practices Act**: gives the **CFO** power to investigate companies, issue cease-and-desist orders, impose penalties, and seek court injunctions. Covers coercion, unfair discrimination (between insureds of the same class), false advertising, misrepresentation, inequitable claim settlement (pay claims in order received).
- **Guaranty Association**: all insurers must participate as a condition of operating in the state; **funded by assessments on insurers**; pays claims of **insolvent** insurers (with limits); **cannot be used in advertising**. **[EXAM]**
- **Rating services**: AM Best, Standard & Poor's, Moody's, Fitch — rate **financial strength / ability to pay claims** (A++ etc.; an "A" is not necessarily the top).
- **Buyer's Guide vs Policy Summary** (p. 25) **[EXAM]**: Guide = **G**eneric/**G**eneral plain-language education (term vs whole life, major med vs HMO; small booklet). Policy **S**ummary = **S**pecific to the policy: cost indexes, comparison figures (larger document).
- Stock companies exist by preference; insureds of a stock company need not own its stock.

## Day 2 — Jun 8 AM (review Ch 1–2; then Ch 3: Law & the Insurance Contract)

### Review additions (Ch 1–2)
- Three lines in the book: life, health, annuities/variable contracts.
- Stock = non-participating (policyholders get no dividends); mutual = participating.
- Medicare = **federally administered**; Social Security = federal; Medicaid = **state-administered** with federal + state funding. **[EXAM]**

### Contract law (Ch 3)
- Valid contract elements, acronym **CLOC**: **C**ompetent parties, **L**egal purpose, **O**ffer & acceptance, **C**onsideration. Consideration = something of value: applicant's = **premium + truthful statements on the application**; insurer's = promise to pay claims. **[EXAM]**
- Insurance-contract characteristics:
  - **Aleatory** — unequal exchange of values; element of chance must exist (small premium vs large benefit; "E.T." memory hook). **[EXAM — asked many ways]**
  - **Adhesion** — take it or leave it; no negotiation.
  - **Unilateral** — only one party's promise is legally enforceable (the insurer's); insured can stop paying and can't be sued. **[EXAM]**
  - **Conditional** — pays only if conditions met (death/disability + proof, e.g., death certificate).
  - **Valued vs indemnity** — valued pays a **stated amount**; acronym **LAD** = **L**ife, **A**D&D, **D**isability income are valued. Medical expense, property/casualty, subrogation = **indemnity** (restore actual loss). **[EXAM]**
  - **Not a personal contract**, but ownership can be transferred by **assignment** (p. 36; insurer must be notified/agree; common with funeral homes).
  - **Utmost good faith** — both parties; all material facts.
- **Insurable interest**: applicant must be subject to loss on the insured's death/illness/disability; must exist **at inception ONLY** (divorce later doesn't void). No insurable interest = void/unenforceable. **[EXAM]**
- **STOLI/IOLI** (stranger/investor-originated life insurance): investors induce (usually elderly) people to take out policies with the investor as beneficiary — questionable/prohibited concept.
- **Warranty** = statement **guaranteed true**; becomes part of the contract; if untrue → grounds for revoking (presumed material). **Representation** = statement **believed true**. **Concealment** = failure to disclose a **known material fact**. Material = would change the insurer's decision. **[EXAM]**
- Agency law: acts of the agent = acts of the principal (insurer); payment to agent = payment to insurer; knowledge of agent = knowledge of insurer.
- Agent authority **[EXAM]**: **Express** = written in the agency contract. **Implied** = not written; what the public reasonably expects / boss knows you do it. **Apparent** = appearance of authority from the **customer's viewpoint** (business cards, rate book) — e.g., fired agent still writing apps = apparent authority.
- **Fiduciary** = position of trust handling others' money; requirements: fit & proper, honest/trustworthy, good business reputation, competent, knows state law, acts in good faith.
- **Waiver** = voluntary relinquishment of a known legal right. **Estoppel** = involuntary — legally **forbidden** from re-asserting a waived right (p. 43). **[EXAM]**
- **Void** = no legal effect, never a contract (illegal purpose; murder-for-hire); neither party can enforce. **Voidable** = valid until a party with the right cancels (e.g., nonpayment; minors).
- Fraud: insurer generally has **2 years (contestability)** to contest; exceptions with no time limit: impersonation, no insurable interest, intent to murder.
- **Parole evidence rule**: oral statements cannot change/override the written contract ("parole" contains O-R-A-L). Get it in writing. **[EXAM]**
- **E&O (errors & omissions) professional liability insurance protects the AGENT** against groundless/false/fraudulent suits (p. 42). **[EXAM]**

## Day 2 — Jun 8 PM (Ch 4: Licensure, Ethics & the Producer + reviews)

- **Unauthorized insurers**: insurers must be authorized/licensed by the state; authorization = **Certificate of Authority (COA)** issued by the **OIR (Office of Insurance Regulation)**. **[EXAM]**
- Producer's **primary responsibility in the application process is to the insurer** (insurance company) — p. 51, "service begins with the application." **[EXAM — trick question]**
- **Misrepresentation**: misleading/inaccurate statements about policy features/benefits — happens mostly **during the one-on-one presentation**; often due to lack of product knowledge. Includes overstating guarantees, claiming dividends/cash values guaranteed, misstating costs, calling an optional rider "required."
- **Defamation**: false/malicious statements injuring another **company's or producer's** reputation or financial condition.
- **Rebating** **[EXAM]**: returning part of the commission (or anything of value, e.g., $100–$200 gift card) to induce purchase — **illegal in Florida** unless done under the company's **filed rebate schedule**: available to ALL insureds in the same **actuarial class**, per schedule, uniformly applied, prominently displayed; can't rebate if insurer prohibits it. Rebate % can't vary by age, sex, race, ethnicity, marital status.
- Actuarial/underwriting classes: preferred / standard / substandard.
- **Twisting** = inducing replacement with **another company's** policy via misleading info (memory hook: a twister/tornado happens *outside*). **Churning** = replacement **within the same company** by the same producer for commission. **Coercion** = forcing the purchase (e.g., tying to a loan). **[EXAM — "which unfair trade practice occurred?" is a favorite]**
- Replacement is legal if it benefits the client, but consider: age/health changes, **new 2-year contestability period**, new fees/expenses, possible loss of upgrades and **grandfathered rights** (any change kills grandfathering); never tell a client to cancel before the new policy is approved.
- **Market conduct (examination)** = essentially an **audit** (e.g., company calls your client to verify the application).
- Policy effective dates **[EXAM]**:
  - Life: policy not effective until **delivered**, signed, and (agent verifies continued good health).
  - **Conditional receipt**: premium paid with the application → if applicant dies during underwriting and would have been approved, the claim pays. No premium = no consideration = no coverage.
  - Health timing: pay with June application → approved → effective July 1; don't pay until July approval → effective August 1 (effective the first of the month after payment+approval).
- Underwriting checks & balances (p. 52): **MIB**, **inspection report** (someone calls the client to verify they're real / answers match), **credit report**.
- **Insuring clause**: on the policy's first page, signed by the company **president and secretary** — the insurer's promise to pay. **[EXAM]**
- Producer license can be suspended/revoked for (p. 74): material untruths on the license application; stating a policy is self-supporting or that projected dividends are guaranteed; implying a policy is sold by an "investment department"; etc.
- Premiums held by a producer = held in a **fiduciary capacity**; never in a personal account. **[EXAM]**
- Insurance is primarily regulated by **state government**; in Florida the **Chief Financial Officer (CFO)** enforces insurance laws. **[EXAM]**
- FL 2-40 requirements: application, age (18+), required licensing procedures — **no law degree**; **citizenship not required**.
- Ethics framing: fiduciary duty, sell to the **need** (financial needs analysis), full disclosure ("cornerstone of product presentation" = **full disclosure** **[EXAM]**), educate before selling, service after the sale, suitability (esp. seniors/Medicare), integrity = what you do when no one is watching. Two most important commitments to the policyowner: obtain/maintain **knowledge** and **educate** the client.

### Ch 15 — Intro to health insurance
- "Health/accident & sickness/health insurance" used interchangeably.
- Three fundamental coverages (no "limited pay health" **[EXAM]**): think **MAD** — **M**edical expense, **A**D&D, **D**isability income.
  - Medical expense: hospital, surgical, physician, outpatient, Medicare supplement, LTC, cancer policies — **reimbursement (indemnity)** basis. **Long-term care ≠ long-term disability.** Medicare does NOT include LTC services.
  - Disability income: replaces **lost income** (a % — never 100%), paid **directly to the insured**, periodically (at least monthly); excludes the medical expenses arising from the disability; protects against "economic death."
  - AD&D: **purest form of accident insurance**, very restrictive, rarely pays, cheap, usually attached/ridered to other policies; lump sums for accidental death or loss of limbs/sight.
- Purchase channels: **individual** (contract between insured and commercial insurer; evidence of insurability may be required; non-participating), **group** (master contract; rated on the group, not the individual — group experience-rating refund is NOT driven by an individual's age/gender **[EXAM]**; participating), **government** (Medicare federal; Medicaid state for low income; OASDI).
- **Renewability provisions** (more insured control = higher premium) **[EXAM]**:
  - **Cancelable**: insurer may cancel anytime with notice (~45 days) — least expensive.
  - **Optionally renewable**: insurer may decline renewal **at the policy anniversary**.
  - **Conditionally renewable**: renewal subject to stated conditions.
  - **Guaranteed renewable**: insurer cannot cancel (if premiums paid) but **may raise premiums by class** — HMOs, PPOs, major medical, cancer policies, Medicare supplements.
  - **Noncancellable ("non-can")**: cannot cancel AND cannot raise premiums — most expensive; mainly disability income for high earners (doctors/nurses).
- Premium **modes**: weekly (smallest payment, highest total cost), monthly, quarterly, semi-annual, annual (usually a discount ≈ one month).
- Premium factors, acronym **"MIX-T"/MIST**: **M**orbidity (health = sickness/disability; mortality = death/life insurance), **I**nterest (insurer invests premiums), e**X**penses, **T**ypes of benefits. Dividends/experience refunds driven mainly by **expenses and claims costs** — usually paid as premium reductions (return of premium, not taxable).
- **Reserves**: money set aside for future claims (regulated; insufficient reserves → insolvent). Premium reserves and loss/claim reserves.
- Health premiums are paid **year-by-year and may experience periodic increases**. **[EXAM]**
- A contract paying "$350/month if totally disabled" = **valued**. **[EXAM]**
- **ACA**: no pre-existing-condition denials (children first 6 months after enactment, then everyone); **no annual or lifetime limits**; children on parents' plan to **age 26**; **employers with 50+ employees must provide coverage or pay ~$2,000/worker/year**; **subsidies for 100–400% of federal poverty level** (income verified with IRS — must file taxes or lose subsidy; over-earning → repay subsidy); **Medicaid expansion to ≤138% FPL** (~$21,597/individual, 2025); standardized/uniform plan presentation (compare apples to apples).

## Day 3 — Jun 9 PM (Ch 16: Health Insurance Providers + reviews)

- **Monoline** insurer = one line only; multi-line = several.
- **Commercial insurers**: reimbursement approach (insured treated, submits bills) + **right of assignment** (benefits paid directly to the provider so the insured needn't pay first — built into most policies, p. 284) **[EXAM]**; freedom to choose providers; offer major medical (deductible + coinsurance).
- **Service providers** (Blue Cross/Blue Shield, HMO/PPO/POS sellers): benefits as **services, not reimbursement**; insureds = **subscribers**; hospitals/physicians contract at stipulated costs. BCBS selling an HMO acts as a service provider, not an insurer. **[EXAM]**
  - **Blue Cross = hospital expenses; Blue Shield = physician/surgical services.** **[EXAM]**
- **HMO**: prepaid — **capitation = fixed amount per head paid to doctors in advance**; in-network only (out-of-network covered only in **emergency/urgent**); choose a **PCP gatekeeper**; **referral required** for specialists; emphasizes **preventive care & early detection**; copays common; **open panel = physicians independent/contracted; closed panel = physicians are HMO employees** (exam tip: open = independent, closed = employee). **[EXAM]**
- **PPO**: contracted providers at **predetermined rates**; paid **fee-for-service after the visit**; **no referral needed**; out-of-network IS covered but acts like major medical (deductible + coinsurance / higher cost); greater flexibility, costs more.
- **POS (point of service)**: HMO/PPO hybrid; highest benefit level in-network (PCP coordinates); out-of-network works like a PPO and the insured **must submit claim forms**; cheaper than PPO, more flexible than HMO.
- **Medicare** (federal; for 65+, **chronic kidney disease**, or after **24 months** receiving Social Security disability): Part **A** = hospital/room & board (room + meals/food) — **compulsory**; Part **B** = doctors, outpatient & miscellaneous — **voluntary** (premium ≈ **$202/month**, usually deducted from the Social Security check); Part **C** = Medicare Advantage (extra benefits like dental/vision); Part **D** = prescription drugs. People without enough work quarters can buy Medicare. **[EXAM — "which part pays X" questions]**
- **Social Security disability (OASDI/SSDI)** **[EXAM]**: must be unable to perform **any substantial gainful work**; condition expected to last **≥12 months or result in death**; **5-month waiting period, benefits begin the 6th month** — and the check arrives the month after it's due (Gina disabled in July → waiting Aug–Dec → January benefit **paid in February**); requires sufficient **work credits/quarters** (no credits → no SSDI; check ssa.gov); **no second waiting period if the same disability recurs within 5 years**.
- **Medicaid**: for the **needy/indigent — no age requirement** (blind, disabled, <21, pregnant/postpartum women, low income); **state-administered, federal matching funds**; **means-tested on income AND assets**; can pay Medicare deductibles/coinsurance for **dual eligibles**. FALSE: "restricts help to people over 65." **[EXAM]**
- **Workers' compensation**: state program; **compulsory**; covers **on-the-job** injury/illness/death only; employee receives benefits **without filing a lawsuit**; **employer-funded (employee contributes nothing)**; benefits regardless of employer negligence; employers may buy a policy, a state plan, or **self-insure (risk retention)**. Health insurance won't pay work-related claims.
- **TRICARE** = health benefits for **active-duty military and families**. **[EXAM]**
- Self-insurance (large corporations, labor unions, fraternals): paperwork handled by **TPA (third-party administrator)** or **ASO (administrative services only)**; a self-insured may add a **minimum premium plan (MPP)** for large/unanticipated (catastrophic) losses.
- **MET (multiple employer trust)**: small employers join a trust to get group rates; **the TRUST holds the master contract** (and "the bucket of cash") — not the individual employers **[EXAM]**; employer must join the trust; may be self-funded or fully insured; members need NOT share an industry.
- **MEWA (multiple employer welfare arrangement)**: a type of MET; members **must share an employment-related common bond / same industry** (unions, trade & professional associations); **tax-exempt**; often self-insured. State-regulated because they act like insurance.
- **DPCA (direct primary care agreement)**: written agreement between primary-care physician and patient (or legal agent/employer), both must sign; flat periodic/monthly fee; **is NOT insurance and does NOT satisfy ACA minimum coverage**; no third-party indemnification — **no surgery/hospitalization/major testing**; doctor bills no insurer.
- PM review extras: deductible = amount insured pays **before benefits begin**; copay = **fixed dollar** amount per covered service; coinsurance = **percentage** shared insurer/insured — coinsurance is the provision that **prevents over-utilization** **[EXAM]**; out-of-pocket maximum ("MOOP") = most the insured pays for covered services in a policy period, then plan pays 100%.

## Day 4 — Jun 10 AM (review; Ch 17: Medical Expense Insurance)

### Review additions
- If asked "waiting period for SSDI" = **5 months**; first **payment** arrives in month 6 (or the following month) — read the question carefully (waiting period vs payment date).
- Health-coverage trio acronym = **MAD** (medical expense / AD&D / disability); valued-contract trio = **LAD**.
- Hospital expense (fixed-rate) policies pay **the insured**, never the hospital.

### Basic plans = "first dollar insurance" (pay first, before any major medical; no deductibles, no coinsurance — if an answer mentions deductible/coinsurance it is NOT basic) **[EXAM]**
Five coverage categories: hospital, surgical, physicians' (non-surgical), nurses' expense, convalescent care.
- **Basic hospital expense**: room & board (room + **food**; daily limit chosen at purchase — higher daily amount = higher premium) + **miscellaneous expenses**: drugs, x-rays, **anesthesia (the substance)**, lab fees, dressings, operating room, supplies. Miscellaneous limit usually a **multiple of the daily room & board** (10–20×). **NOT covered: the anesthesiologist (a person), physicians' bedside visits, doctors' services, nursing.** Trap wording: "the administration of anesthesia" = a person administering = NOT covered; "anesthesia" = covered. **[EXAM — multiple questions]**
- **Basic surgical expense** covers the **people** — acronym **SAP**: **S**urgeon, **A**nesthesiologist, **P**ost-operative care. Three payment methods **[EXAM]**:
  1. **Surgical schedule** — flat dollar list per procedure (excess is insured's).
  2. **Reasonable & customary** — based on average charges in the geographic area (excess is insured's).
  3. **Relative value scale** — points × conversion factor (60 units × $10 = **$600**).
- **Basic physicians' (non-surgical) expense**: office visits / non-surgical doctor care, stated amount per visit.
- **Nurses' expense**: private-duty nursing (RN/LPN) on doctor's orders while hospitalized.
- **Convalescent care**: daily benefit in a skilled nursing facility after hospital discharge; NOT custodial care or rest cures.

### Major medical
- Keywords signaling major medical: **major, comprehensive, reimbursement, flat deductible, right of assignment, commercial insurer**.
- Covers room & board + extras, nursing, blood, oxygen, prosthetics, surgery; group or individual; **excludes cosmetic surgery**; dental only if from accident; (ACA) no pre-existing exclusions or annual/lifetime maximums.
- **Supplementary major medical**: pairs with a basic plan (basic pays first; major medical picks up the rest). **Comprehensive major medical**: one standalone policy (exam tip: supplementary = with basic; comprehensive = standalone, uses a FLAT deductible). **[EXAM]**
- **Three deductible types (flat, corridor, integrated — stop-loss is NOT a deductible)** **[EXAM]**:
  - **Flat**: stated amount the insured pays first ($500 of $2,000 → insurer works from $1,500). **Family deductible** ≈ 3× individual; if 3 family members each meet it, the 4th member's expenses have no deductible.
  - **Corridor**: with supplementary plans — basic pays first; after basic is **exhausted**, the insured pays the full deductible (the "corridor" between basic and major medical), then major medical pays. Memory: hallway — basic | insured | major.
  - **Integrated**: amounts paid by the basic plan **count toward/satisfy** the major-medical deductible (if basic pays ≥ the deductible, the insured owes no deductible; if basic pays less, insured makes up the difference).
- **Coinsurance**: common splits **80/20 and 75/25** (insurer pays the larger share); applied **after the deductible**. Worked example: $502,000 bill, $2,000 deductible → 80% of $500,000 = $400,000 insurer; insured pays $2,000 + $100,000 = **$102,000** (75/25 → insured $127,000). Circle who the question asks about (insured vs insurer). **[EXAM — math]**
- **Stop-loss**: caps the insured's coinsurance outlay (commonly after $1,000–$2,000 paid), then insurer pays **100%** of covered expenses; deductible still applies (stop-loss = coinsurance only). **Maximum out-of-pocket** includes the deductible (plan pays 100% after). $0-premium ACA plans = higher stop-loss/MOOP. Practical tip: schedule major procedures early in the calendar year.
- Pre-existing conditions: condition existing before the effective date; after the exclusion period it's no longer "pre-existing"; ACA-compliant plans can't exclude them at all.
- **HDHP (high-deductible health plan)**: very low premium / high deductible; for healthy people; paired with an **HSA**.
- **HSA** **[EXAM]**: employee-owned (portable debit card); contributions **pre-tax/tax-deductible**, **tax-free growth**, **tax-free withdrawals for qualified medical expenses**; **non-qualified withdrawals = income tax + 10% penalty**; **at death, ownership transfers to the spouse TAX-FREE** (p. 303 — flagged exam question). Requirements (from app/HSA rules): HDHP, no other first-dollar coverage, not on Medicare (HSAs are for under-65), not a dependent.
- **HRA**: **employer-funded only**; reimburses deductibles/copays/coinsurance; employer sets limits; rollover at employer's discretion. **FSA**: employee-funded via pre-tax **salary reduction**; use-it-or-lose-it.
- **Hospital indemnity / fixed-rate policies** (p. 304): pay a stated **daily/weekly/monthly amount** while hospitalized, **paid directly to the insured**, regardless of actual charges (AFLAC-style supplement); premiums least affected by rising medical costs. Fixed-rate = "a fixed amount per day for room and board." **[EXAM]**
- **Limited risk / dread disease / critical illness**: one peril (cancer, heart disease, stroke); usually **lump sum**; prohibited in some states (fear-based sales).
- **Accident-only**: injuries from accidents, never sickness; may pay death/disability/dismemberment/medical.
- **Short-term medical expense**: temporary bridge coverage, **90 or 180 days** (renewals capped ~36 months); **NOT ACA-compliant** → may exclude pre-existing conditions and impose annual/lifetime limits.

## Day 4 — Jun 10 PM (Ch 18: Disability Income Insurance + reviews)

- Purpose: replace earnings, protect against **"economic death"**; pays a **percentage of pre-disability earnings**, never 100%; a **valued** contract (flat stated amount known at purchase). Individual policies = **flat dollar amount** (e.g., $5,000/mo); group = **% of earnings** (e.g., 60%).
- **Occupational** coverage = on-the-job AND off-the-job. **Non-occupational** = off-the-job only (cheaper — workers' comp already covers the job). **[EXAM]**
- Definitions of total disability:
  - **Own occupation**: can't perform YOUR OWN job — easier to qualify, more favorable, more expensive (surgeon who can teach still collects). **[EXAM scenarios: surgeon/dentist]**
  - **Any occupation**: can't perform any job suited by **education, training, or experience** — more restrictive. **"Job interest" is NEVER a criterion.** **[EXAM]**
  - Course note: permanent inability to perform own occupation = **permanent TOTAL** disability even if other work is possible.
- **Presumptive disability**: automatic total disability, no further proof — loss of **sight, hearing, speech, or two limbs**; usually lump sum.
- **Partial disability**: can work but not full duties/full time; usually requires prior total disability. Two benefit methods **[EXAM]**:
  - **Flat amount** = **50% of the total disability benefit** (typical of individual policies). Note: % applies to the BENEFIT amount, not salary.
  - **Residual** = proportional to **lost income** (40% earnings drop → 40% of benefit); common in group plans; requires physician care and often a minimum income loss (~20–25%). Exam hook: "residual/partial % of lost income" → residual; working-but-earning-less scenarios are best answered **residual**.
- **Accidental means vs accidental bodily injury (results)** **[EXAM]**:
  - **Accidental means**: the CAUSE must be accidental — *fell* off the ladder = covered; *jumped* = not covered. Avoid policies with "accidental means" — harder to collect.
  - **Accidental bodily injury (results)**: only the RESULT must be accidental — jumping off and getting hurt IS covered. Results = broader/better coverage.
- **Probationary period**: one-time, starts at policy effective date, usually **15–30 days**; applies to **SICKNESS ONLY — never accidents** (accident during it is still covered); excludes pre-existing illness / prevents adverse selection. **[EXAM — repeated]**
- **Elimination (waiting) period**: a **time deductible**; chosen at inception; **begins on the date of disability (injury/diagnosis), not the claim date**; no benefits paid during it and **no retroactive payment for it**; **longer elimination = lower premium** (inverse relationship — p. 314); usually ≥30 days (range 1 week–1 year). May apply to sickness only depending on the policy. 90-day elimination, disabled Jan 1 → benefits begin **April 1**. **[EXAM]**
- **Benefit period** = maximum time benefits are paid. Individual **short-term: 6 months–2 years**; **long-term: 5/10/20/30 years or to age 65/retirement** (longer = pricier). Get both STD and LTD at work if offered; LTD picks up when STD ends. Benefits must be paid **at least monthly**. **[EXAM]**
- **Delayed disability**: disability emerging after the accident is covered if within the stated window — **30/60/90 days**.
- **Recurrent disability** **[EXAM]**: if the same disability returns within the stated period (commonly **≤6 months** after returning to work) it is a **continuation** — benefits resume immediately, **no new elimination period** (Rachel: Jan 15–Apr 15, relapse Sep 1, return lasted <6 months → continuation). Return to work **longer than 6 months** (or full recovery, per the course's Marcus question) → **new disability: new elimination period AND fresh benefit period**. Know your policy's stated window.
- **Non-disabling injury benefit**: injury that doesn't qualify as a disability → pays the **actual cost of medical treatment**, limited to a percentage of the weekly/monthly benefit, paid in lieu of other benefits.
- **Riders** (all cost extra) **[EXAM]**:
  - **Waiver of premium**: premiums waived during total disability; 3–6 month wait, often **retroactive** (premiums refunded); generally **drops at age 60–65**; **included with guaranteed renewable and noncancellable DI policies**; found in **disability income (and life) — NOT medical expense** policies (the "applies to both medical expense and DI" statement is FALSE).
  - **Social Insurance Supplement / Social Security rider**: pays extra while Social Security benefits are pending, denied, or smaller than the rider amount.
  - **COLA rider**: benefit indexed to the **Consumer Price Index (CPI)**; adjusts at each policy anniversary (~3–4%).
  - **Guaranteed insurability / future increase option** (on DI and LTC policies): buy more coverage later **without evidence of insurability**; subject to an **earnings test** (must show increased income); must exercise **before age 65**.
- Scenario answers from the review: comprehensive major medical → **flat** deductible; $150/day while hospitalized → **hospital indemnity**; cancer lump sum → **limited risk**; healthy 30-yr-old, low premium/high deductible + HSA → **HDHP**; 180-day non-ACA policy → **short-term medical**; sickness on day 10 of a 30-day probationary period → not paid; broken leg in the same window → **paid** (accident); blind in both eyes → **presumptive**; insurer pays premiums while disabled → **waiver of premium**; income doubled at 40, more coverage without exam → **guaranteed insurability**; inflation protection → **COLA**; back injury lifting at work + non-occupational policy → **NOT covered**.
