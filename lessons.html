Purpose of Life and Health Insurance (Unit 1)

Fundamentals of Insurance

Insurance exists to answer one question: "Who pays when something goes wrong?" Rather than one person bearing the full financial weight of a death, injury, or illness, insurance lets a large group share the burden. This idea rests on two pillars.

  • Risk pooling (loss sharing) — a group of people each contributes a small amount into a common fund. When one member suffers a loss, the fund pays. The loss shifts from one person to many, making it manageable for everyone.
  • Law of large numbers — a mathematical principle: the larger the group of similar risks combined, the more accurately an insurer can predict future losses. A pool of 100,000 similar-age drivers gives far better loss predictions than a pool of 50. This predictability is what makes insurance commercially viable.
Exam Tip: If the exam asks "what gives insurance its certainty," the answer is the law of large numbers. If it asks "what spreads the financial burden," the answer is risk pooling. They work together but test differently.

Risk: The Two Classes

Risk in insurance means uncertainty regarding financial loss. Not all risk is insurable — you need to know which kind is which.

  • Speculative risk — involves the chance of loss, no change, OR gain. Think gambling, investing in the stock market, or starting a business. Because there's a potential for profit, speculative risks are NOT insurable.
  • Pure risk — involves only the chance of loss or no loss, with no possibility of gain. An injury from an accident is pure risk: you won't profit if it doesn't happen, and you lose if it does. Only pure risks are insurable.

Insurance is not designed for the insured to profit — it restores the insured to the same financial condition they were in before the loss. This concept is called indemnity.

Exam Tip: The study guide uses the ARRT mnemonic for risk handling (Avoid, Reduce, Retain, Transfer), but the exam often frames the question as: 'Purchasing insurance is the most effective way of ______ risk.' The answer is always transferring. Risk retention is also called self-insuring.

Perils and Hazards

A peril is the cause of a loss. In life insurance, the peril is death. In health insurance, the peril is sickness or injury. A hazard is a condition that increases the likelihood of a loss occurring.

  • Physical hazard — a tangible condition that increases risk: icy walkways, broken stairs, wet floors, a person's poor health or dangerous occupation.
  • Moral hazard — arises from character defects like dishonesty. A person who files false insurance claims or commits arson for insurance money presents a moral hazard.
  • Morale hazard — arises from carelessness or indifference because one has insurance. Smoking, not wearing a helmet, or leaving doors unlocked because "insurance will cover it" are morale hazards.
Exam Tip: The study guide groups moral and morale hazards together, but the exam draws a sharp distinction. Moral = intentional dishonesty (fraud). Morale = carelessness (indifference). A classic exam trap is listing both and asking which involves dishonesty — that's moral hazard.

Handling Risk

There are four methods of handling risk, remembered with the mnemonic ARRT:

  • Risk avoidance — eliminating the exposure entirely. If you never go skydiving, you avoid the risk of a skydiving injury. Not always practical.
  • Risk reduction — taking steps to lower the probability or severity of loss. Installing a car alarm reduces theft risk. Wearing a seatbelt reduces injury severity.
  • Risk retention — accepting the financial responsibility yourself. Choosing a high deductible or not buying insurance at all means you retain risk. Also called self-insuring.
  • Risk transference — shifting the financial risk to another party. Buying insurance is the primary example — the insurer assumes the financial risk in exchange for premium payments.
Exam Tip: On the exam, remember that insurance is always classified as risk transference, never risk reduction. Installing sprinklers reduces risk; buying a fire policy transfers it. If both are answer choices, pick the one that matches the question's wording.

Elements of Insurable Risk

Not every risk is insurable. For a risk to qualify as insurable, it must meet six criteria:

  • Fortuitous (chance) — the loss must be accidental and outside the policyholder's control. Intentional losses are not insurable.
  • Definite and measurable — the loss must be definite as to cause, time, place, and amount so the insurer can quantify it.
  • Statistically predictable — the insurer must be able to use statistics to predict the frequency and severity of losses.
  • Not catastrophic — the risk must have thresholds at which insurers can expect losses to stop. Wars and widespread floods typically fail this test.
  • Large number of similar exposure units — the insurer needs a large pool of similar risks to apply the law of large numbers.
  • Randomly selected — the group covered and the exposures must be chosen at random to prevent adverse selection.
Exam Tip: A popular exam question: 'Which of the following does NOT constitute an insurable risk?' The answer is typically 'the loss must be catastrophic' — because insurability requires the loss to be non-catastrophic. Watch the wording carefully.

Adverse Selection

Adverse selection is the tendency of higher-risk individuals to seek insurance more actively than lower-risk individuals. A person who knows they have a health condition is more motivated to buy health insurance than a perfectly healthy person. Left unchecked, adverse selection would flood the insurance pool with high-risk applicants, driving up costs for everyone.

Insurers combat adverse selection through underwriting — evaluating each applicant's risk profile and assigning appropriate premiums or declining coverage when the risk is too great.

Exam Tip: The study guide defines adverse selection but doesn't explain the remedy. The exam asks: 'How do insurers control adverse selection?' The answer is underwriting.

Human Life Value

Human life value is a person's economic worth measured by the total future earnings that would have been devoted to their family. When determining how much life insurance a person needs, the human life value approach calculates the present value of the income stream the family would lose if the breadwinner died.

Exam Tip: The needs approach is the other common method for calculating life insurance needs — it adds up what the family would actually need (mortgage payoff, education funds, living expenses) rather than projecting lost income. Know both for the exam, but the study guide emphasizes human life value.

  • What's the difference between a peril, a physical hazard, a moral hazard, and a morale hazard?
  • Name the four methods of handling risk using the ARRT mnemonic.
